How Aware People Are of the Problem: Statistics

To better understand the scale of the e-waste problem, it's important to look at the numbers. Statistics help us see how much electronic waste is produced, how little is recycled, and how the problem is growing every year. In this section, we show some key data to highlight the urgency of taking action.

82%

Total E-Waste Generated (2022): 62 million tonnes (Mt)

22.3%

Recycling Rate (2022) Only this percent of e-waste was formally collected and recycled, leaving approximately $62 billion worth of recoverable materials unaccounted for.

20%

Projected Recycling Rate (2030) Expected to decline to 20% if current trends continue.

What is known and what is not known?

People don’t always have a lot of knowledge, especially younger or older generations, because of that it’s important to make them aware

What is known:

  • Electronic devices become waste when they no longer work.
  • It's important not to throw electronics in the regular trash.
  • Some stores or collection centers accept old devices for recycling.
  • The use of technology is constantly increasing.
  • E-waste can harm the environment.

What is not known:

  • Only a small percentage of global e-waste is actually recycled.
  • Electronic devices contain toxic materials like mercury and lead.
  • Much of the e-waste is illegally exported to developing countries.
  • Many devices can have a longer life with simple repairs.
  • There are specific laws and regulations about e-waste disposal.

How the news and programs talk about it

Television and streaming platforms have played an important role in raising awareness about the e-waste problem. Through documentaries, news reports, and educational programs, they show how electronic waste is affecting the planet and the people involved in its disposal. These programs help viewers understand where e-waste goes, who is affected by it, and what can be done to reduce it.

Some programs:

  • The E-Waste Tragedy:A documentary that investigates the illegal export of electronic waste from Europe to Africa and Asia.
  • Frontline: Ghana – Digital Dumping Ground:A PBS episode showing how e-waste from developed countries ends up in Ghana.
  • Broken: Where Your Electronics Go to Die: (Netflix) – An episode from the Broken series that explores the dark side of the electronics industry.
  • EARTHxTV: Electronic Wasteland: A documentary that shows the impact of e-waste on people and the environment, especially in developing countries.
